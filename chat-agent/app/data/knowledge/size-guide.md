# Size Guide

This guide explains how to measure yourself, how to read our size charts, and how to choose between sizes when you fall on the boundary. The advice applies to most apparel categories on the storefront. Specific products may include category-specific notes on the product page; those notes take priority over this general guide when they disagree.

## How to Measure

You only need a soft measuring tape and a mirror. If you do not have a tape, use a string and measure the string against a ruler. Wear thin clothing or measure over your underwear so the tape can sit flush against your body without being squeezed.

- **Chest.** Measure around the fullest part of your chest, keeping the tape level under your armpits and across your shoulder blades.
- **Waist.** Measure around your natural waistline, which is the narrowest part of your torso, usually just above the navel. Do not pull tight.
- **Hips.** Stand with your feet together and measure around the fullest part of your hips and rear.
- **Inseam.** Measure from the top of your inner thigh down to the bottom of your ankle along the inside of your leg.
- **Sleeve.** With your arm slightly bent, measure from the center back of your neck, across your shoulder, and down to your wrist.

Keep the tape parallel to the floor for chest, waist, and hip measurements. A tape that tilts produces a number that is too small.

## Reading the Size Chart

Each product page includes a size chart with the body measurements that fit each size. Compare your measurements to the chart, not to a size you wore in another brand. Brands and even product lines within a brand fit differently, and a size that fits in one cut may be too tight or too loose in another.

Where two of your measurements span two sizes, prioritize the measurement that determines the silhouette of the garment:

- **Tops.** Chest is usually the deciding measurement. For oversized cuts, prioritize comfort over chart precision.
- **Trousers and shorts.** Waist is usually the deciding measurement, with hips as a secondary check.
- **Dresses.** Use chest for fitted bodices and hips for fitted skirts.
- **Outerwear.** Chest is the primary measurement, but allow extra room for layering underneath.

If your measurement falls exactly on a size boundary, use the Between Sizes rule below.

## Between Sizes

If you are between sizes, choose the larger size for relaxed outdoor layering, technical jackets, and any product the description calls slim or athletic. The larger size gives you room for a midlayer or for sleeves to clear a watch.

Choose the smaller size for stretch-heavy items, knits, and items the description calls relaxed, oversized, or boxy. These pieces are designed with extra ease, and going up a size often makes the garment look unstructured.

When unsure, the larger size is the safer default. Tightness is harder to fix than excess length, which can usually be tailored.

## Common Fit Notes

- **Slim cut.** Closer to the body. Use a measurement that matches your body without ease, and consider sizing up if you prefer movement.
- **Regular cut.** Standard ease. Use the chart directly.
- **Relaxed cut.** Designed with extra ease. Use the chart directly and resist sizing up.
- **Tall sizes.** Add roughly 5 cm to the standard length on tops and 7.5 cm on trousers. Available on selected products.
- **Petite sizes.** Subtract roughly 5 cm to the standard length on tops and 7.5 cm on trousers. Available on selected products.

## Footwear

Foot length and width vary by region. Measure the length of each foot in centimeters by standing on a piece of paper, marking the heel and the longest toe, and measuring between the marks. Use the longer foot.

Convert your foot length to our footwear size using the chart on the product page. For boots and outdoor shoes, add 0.5 cm of toe room. For dress shoes, use the exact length unless the product page recommends otherwise.

## Care for Sizing Longevity

Wash and dry per the care label to preserve the fit you bought. Hot water and machine drying can shrink natural fibers by 3 to 5 percent on the first wash. Stretch fabrics relax over time and may feel more generous after a few wears.

If a garment shrinks beyond the manufacturer tolerance on the first wash, it is eligible for return under the standard return policy. Include photographs of the care label and the affected garment when you start the return.

## Body Shape Considerations

Bodies are not perfectly proportional, and standard charts assume one. If your shoulders are wider than your hips, consider one size up on tops and your charted size on bottoms. If your hips are wider than your waist, consider one size up on bottoms and your charted size on tops, then have the waistband taken in by a tailor.

Stretch fabrics, drawstrings, and elasticated waistbands tolerate proportional differences best, and they are good defaults when you fall between sizes.
